\section{User Behavior in Disentangled Representation Space}\label{sec:experiments}

emerges for them? These questions bring us to the

%%% 7.1 From Representation to Behavior %%%
\subsection{From Representation to Behavior}

In a disentangled representation space, a user can act with a clear sense
of what each direction does: what it changes, and what it leaves
untouched. One direction---the genre-ness coordinate $u$ (here, music
genre)---carries them across genres; the other moves them along the
atmosphere coordinate $v$. And the latter---the atmosphere---escapes language: it is felt rather than named. How do users move
in such a space, and what does the journey become for them? That is what
this section sets out to observe.

Let us begin by looking at what two participants did, exploring songs
for a personal playlist with the music discovery system from
Section~\ref{sec:systems}. Figure~\ref{fig:exploration_behavior} shows
their trajectories through the multi-floor library across a three-phase
session.

In the first phase, when rabbit holes were not made visible, exploration
stayed within a few favored genres. In the second phase, when the same
representation was equipped with rabbit-hole cues, vertical movement
appeared: lines now span genres at similar atmosphere coordinates. In the
third phase, with the cues removed, the participants did not return to
where they had been; they continued moving toward other floors.

These two participants are not exceptional. Trajectories of similar
character appeared across the cohort, and similar voices were heard in
the post-experiment interviews. We now turn to the cohort as a whole.


%%% 7.2 Observed User Behavior %%%
\subsection{Observed User Behavior}


\subsubsection{User Study Protocol}\label{subsubsec:protocol}

The user study consisted of two experiments answering different
questions about the same space. Experiment~1 asked whether the
representation alone---with the interface held fixed---changes how
users explore. Experiment~2 asked how exploration moves as the
navigational scaffolding becomes available and is then withdrawn.

Experiment~1 had each participant explore two libraries in turn. The
two were visually identical but built on different models: the
proposed disentangled one (P) against an alternative (C1) in which
each genre carries its own atmosphere axis (e.g., orchestra/piano
within Classical).
Experiment~2 placed users inside a single disentangled library (P)
and observed how their exploration moved as the navigational
scaffolding---the rabbit-hole cues and the cross-floor alignment of
the atmosphere coordinate---became available and was then withdrawn.
The model itself stayed the same throughout; we rotated $\mathcal{V}$
independently per genre, so that switching genres no longer foretells
where each atmosphere lies (C2).

In total, 135 participants took part (63 and 72 across the two
experiments, ages 18--62). Each was asked to explore songs and build a
personal playlist titled \textit{`Fresh Finds'}, with the instruction
to behave naturally: ``not a competition for playlist size, but an
observation of your usual discovery process.'' Sessions ended with a
questionnaire and a brief interview. Three behavioral indicators were
recorded: \textit{genre entropy} $H_g$, breadth across genre floors;
\textit{atmosphere entropy} $H_a$, breadth along the atmosphere
coordinate $v$; and \textit{exploration chain length}, the length of
consecutive moves within a small atmosphere distance. Full procedure,
metric definitions, and statistical tests are given in
Appendix~\ref{app:user_study}. The study was approved by the authors'
institutional research ethics committee.

Figure~\ref{fig:behavioral_measurements1} compares the two breadth
measures between systems~P and~C1 in Experiment~1. Two observations
appear together: in system~P, behavior was broader
\textit{across} genre floors and narrower \textit{along} the
atmosphere coordinate---both in what participants played
(exploration; panels a, c) and in what they marked (discovery; panels b, d). The two
movements occurred in the same participants at the same time, under
both presentation orders.

This is what we saw earlier in the trajectories of two participants;
here it is, across the cohort. It is also what the representation was
designed to make available: a coordinate common to all genres yet
independent of any.


\subsubsection{Sustained Cross-Genre Exploration}

Figure~\ref{fig:chain_length} turns to Experiment~2, where the same
library was traversed in three phases (C2~$\to$~P~$\to$~C2) as the
meaning of the space became visible and was then withheld. The figure
overlays the exploration chains of all 72~participants---each dot a
chain, measured by consecutive moves within a small atmosphere distance.

In the P phase, mean chain length remains modest, but the distribution
shows a pronounced long tail. Some chains extend over dozens of songs,
moving across genre floors while staying at a similar atmosphere
coordinate (red dots).

It is worth dwelling on what this says. We cannot prove that $v$
represents affect itself---no behavioral data could settle that. What
we can say is that users stayed along the same $v$, song after song.
The coordinate carried meaning for them: the path along a rabbit hole
was, for these listeners, far from arbitrary.

What we saw earlier in two participants now appears across the cohort,
aligning with the genre expansion with atmosphere focus seen in
Experiment~1. Perhaps one might put it this way: through perceiving
the meaning of the representation, users were afforded cross-genre
exploration along a resonant atmosphere axis.

The previous subsection observed what users did from the outside. What
did they make of it?

The questionnaire (Figure~\ref{fig:user_experience_evaluation}) gathers
what participants reported feeling, in twenty Likert items grouped into
six aspects---four adapted from the Serendipitous Digital
Environment Scale~\cite{McCayPeet20181} and two developed for this
study. Across both experiments, ratings spoke of finding it easier to
discover (A: ease of discovery), of noticing the cues that drew their
attention (C: trigger-rich; D: highlights triggers), of seeing
connections among items (E: enables connections), and of learning
about their own preferences (F: discovering preferences).
Unexpectedness (B: leads to the unexpected) was rated about the same;
interviews suggested why---in a foreseeable space, discoveries arrived
less as surprise than as something anticipated.

Post-experiment interviews echoed this in many ways. Recurring themes
included finding tastes in genres they would not normally enter,
noticing that what they liked spanned genres rather than belonged
to one, and recognizing why a piece from one floor resonated with a
piece from another. Most striking was how often participants spoke of
discovering not just music but themselves: of finding that their
preferences lived along an atmosphere axis rather than within any
genre, of putting into words what they had previously only felt. Each
described it in their own way; what they described was strikingly
similar.

It seems that when humans are given a well-formed representation
space with signposts, they tend to explore---each in their own
direction---and discover themselves.
