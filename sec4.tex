%%%%% Section 4. Realizing the Multi-Floor Library: Optimal Transport on a Fiber Bundle %%%%%
\section{Realizing the Multi-Floor Library: Optimal Transport on a Fiber Bundle}
\label{sec:modeling}

Section~\ref{sec:library_universe_model} cast the vision: the multi-galaxy
curved universe is to be turned into the multi-floor library. We carry it
out here, at the \textbf{second of the five levels---the Mathematical}.

In machine learning, this transformation is called \textbf{disentanglement},
and it is genuinely difficult~\cite{Locatello20197247}: between any two
galaxies an infinite number of rabbit holes can be drawn, and no algorithm
can choose among them until we define what makes a correspondence
\textit{good}. This forces us to ask: \textit{What is the right rabbit hole?}
The work begins not with the algorithm, but with the representation---asking
what, precisely, we want it to look like.


%%% Subsection 4.1 What representation do we want? %%%%%
\subsection{What representation do we want?}\label{subsec:what-representation}

% --- (1) Setup: body data, genre label, coordinates ---
Let us first formalize the situation. We are given, for each item $n$,
body data $\mathbf{x}_n \in \mathcal{X} = \mathbb{R}^D$ in the body-data
universe of Section~\ref{subsec:multi_galaxy}, together with a genre
label $c_n$ as descriptor.

% --- (2) (u,v) coordinates first, then U×V ---
What we want is a coordinate pair $(u_{c_n},\, v_n)$ for each item:
$u_{c_n}$ identifying which floor the item occupies---its
genre-ness---and $v_n$ its position within that floor---its atmosphere.
Together, these coordinates span the multi-floor library of
Section~\ref{subsec:multi_floor_library}, made mathematical as a
low-dimensional space $\mathcal{U} \times \mathcal{V}$, where
$\mathcal{U}$ is the genre-ness space and $\mathcal{V}$ the atmosphere
space.

% --- (3) Φ: both rabbit holes and floors ---
The two are related by an embedding map
\[
    \Phi: \mathcal{U} \times \mathcal{V} \to \mathcal{X},
\]
which places each coordinate pair $(u, v)$ into the body-data universe
(Figure~\ref{fig:library_universe}). For each $v$, the curve
$\Phi(\cdot, v)$ traces one rabbit hole through the universe; for each
genre $c$, the curve $\Phi(u_c, \cdot)$ traces its floor. Our goal is
to assign each item $n$ coordinates $(u_{c_n}, v_n)$---where $v$ is
common across all genres yet independent of any---and to find the map
$\Phi$ that traces the rabbit holes.

% --- (4) Provably impossible → forces the right question ---
This goal cannot be reached by computation alone. Disentanglement
without further specification is \textbf{provably}
impossible~\cite{Locatello20197247}: infinitely many $\Phi$ satisfy
the formal setup, and data alone cannot tell us which one draws the
rabbit holes we want. This is not a technical inconvenience---it forces
us to ask what makes one correspondence better than another.

%%% Subsection 4.2 The goodness of a rabbit hole %%%%%
\subsection{The goodness of a rabbit hole}\label{subsec:OT-hypothesis}

% --- Para A: 2-galaxy case + Miles & Debussy ---
Let us start from the two-galaxy case. Consider jazz and classical:
two galaxies in the universe, sitting far apart. We wish to draw
rabbit holes between them, pairing items that share atmospheric
character across the genre gap. Miles Davis's contemplative
piece in the jazz galaxy and Debussy's contemplative piece in the
classical galaxy---these we want paired, the rabbit hole connecting
them (Figure~\ref{fig:two_category_ot}).

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{fig/fig4.pdf}
    \caption{
        \textbf{Resolving the \textit{distant yet close} paradox through optimal transport.}
        Miles Davis's piece (orange) and Debussy's piece (red) lie far apart in body-data space $\mathbb{R}^D$, yet the optimal transport map (gray lines) reveals their implicit closeness: they occupy corresponding positions within their respective genre distributions (jazz, upper; classical, lower). The red line marks the \textbf{rabbit hole} connecting these pieces.
    }
    \label{fig:two_category_ot}
\end{figure}

% --- Para B: §4.1 復習 + 独立性の限界 ---
Recall what we asked of $v$ in
Section~\ref{subsec:what-representation}: an atmosphere coordinate
\emph{common across all genres yet independent of any}. The word
\emph{independent} here is the statistical one---$v$ must carry no
information about the genre $u$. Yet statistical independence
alone does not determine $\Phi$. Infinitely many independent
$\Phi$ exist, all satisfying the formal
setup~\cite{Locatello20197247}; data alone cannot tell us which one
draws the rabbit holes we want. Genre labels are our only descriptor;
no explicit supervision on $v$ is available.

% --- Para C: inductive bias + 中核問い + われわれの答え予告 ---
Independence is necessary but not sufficient. We must add a second
condition---an inductive bias---that singles out, among the
infinitely many independent $\Phi$, the one we seek. The bias is not
ours to invent freely: it must be the one the structure we wish to
recover actually requires.

What is this inductive bias? To this single question, the entire
paper is sharpened. Our answer:
\begin{quote}
    \begin{hypothesis}[Optimal Transport Hypothesis]\label{hyp:OT}
        The goodness of a correspondence between two galaxies is the
        cost of optimally transporting one galaxy onto the other. We
        take the right rabbit hole---the right correspondence---to be
        the one realizing this minimum.
    \end{hypothesis}
\end{quote}
% --- Para 5+6: Monge unpacking (concretely と統合) ---
To unpack this, picture two galaxies of equal total mass---you may read
mass as probability normalized to one. A correspondence transports the
mass of one galaxy onto the other; the \textit{transport cost} is the
total mass-weighted squared distance moved. We take minimizing this
transport cost as our definition of the right rabbit hole. Concretely, if each galaxy holds $N=1{,}000$
items of equal indivisible mass, the hypothesis pairs them one-to-one
to minimize the sum of squared moving distances---every item finds
exactly one counterpart, none unpaired, none paired twice.

% --- Para 7: Three reasons ---
Why is this the right bias? Three reasons.
\textit{First}, it gives both statistical independence and uniqueness
in a single stroke~\cite{villani2009optimal,peyre2019computational}:
among all independent $\Phi$, optimal transport singles out the one of
\emph{shortest correspondence}---paired items as close as possible.
\textit{Second}---and this is the inductive bias we sought---shortest
correspondence is precisely the structure the rabbit hole requires:
items that share atmosphere, however distant in genre, are pulled as
close as the transport allows.
\textit{Third}, optimal transport preserves each galaxy's internal
geometry: close items pair with close items, peripheral with
peripheral~\cite{villani2009optimal}.

% --- Para 8: Returning to Miles & Debussy + relational identity ---
Returning to Miles Davis and Debussy
(Figure~\ref{fig:two_category_ot}): the optimal transport map sends
Miles Davis's piece in the jazz galaxy to a position in the classical
galaxy, and Debussy's piece occupies precisely that position. They are
paired not because they sound alike---by direct feature distance they
do not---but because they occupy \textit{corresponding positions within
    their respective galaxies}, each at the contemplative, atmospheric
region of its own genre. The pairing is not pointwise similarity, but
\emph{relational identity}: each is, within its own galaxy, what the
other is within theirs. This is the rabbit hole connecting them.

% --- Para 9: Bridge to §4.3 ---
This establishes the goodness criterion for two galaxies.
Section~\ref{subsec:OTFB-model} extends it to $K$ galaxies.


%%% Subsection 4.3 A bundle of rabbit holes %%%%%
\subsection{A bundle of rabbit holes}\label{subsec:OTFB-model}

% --- (1) 2-cat → K-cat への拡張動機 ---
The two-galaxy case shows the principle, but real collections carry many
genres---jazz, classical, rock, electronic, folk, $\ldots$ $K$ galaxies
in all. To build the multi-floor library we must extend the optimal
transport hypothesis from two galaxies to $K$, with all rabbit holes
consistent with one another.

% --- (2) 拡張条件: 全ペア OT + 共通 v ---
The extension comes in two parts. First, every pair of galaxies is
aligned by optimal transport, as in the two-galaxy case above. Second,
we equip each galaxy with a common low-dimensional \textit{atmosphere
    coordinate} $v$: items connected by a rabbit hole share the same
value of $v$, regardless of which galaxy they belong to.

% --- (3) Manifold + bundle of smooth curves ---
Under these conditions, each galaxy becomes a smooth manifold. A rabbit
hole is no longer a single line segment: it is a continuous curve
threading every galaxy, visiting the points sharing a common $v$. We
treat the genre-ness coordinate as continuous, with unobserved
genres filling between the observed ones. Each rabbit hole then
runs as a smooth, shortest curve through them all. The full collection
of rabbit holes forms a \textit{bundle of smooth curves} tying the
galaxies together (Figure~\ref{fig:library_universe}, right).

% --- (4) fiber bundle → OT-FB + multi-floor library として再認識 ---
In mathematics, a geometric structure consisting of manifolds tied
together by a bundle of curves is called a \textit{fiber bundle}.
Because ours is constructed through optimal transport, we call it the
\textit{optimal transport fiber bundle}, or \textbf{OT-FB}. This is
precisely the multi-floor library specified in
Section~\ref{subsec:what-representation}
(Figure~\ref{fig:library_universe}, left): the genre-ness coordinate
$u$ identifies the floor, and the atmosphere coordinate $v$ the
location on it.

% --- (5) 締めくくり: well-defined mathematical form の獲得 ---
Serendipity's elusive nature, named at the opening of this paper, has
now acquired a well-defined mathematical form. \textit{Distant yet
close} is no longer a paradox; it is the local geometry of the
bundle. Section~\ref{sec:computational} turns to learning this bundle
from data.
