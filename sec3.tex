\section{The Multi-Floor Library and the Multi-Galaxy Curved Universe: A Vision}\label{sec:library_universe_model}

Section~\ref{sec:serendipity} established that the distant-yet-close paradox
calls for a disentangled representation. We carry it now to the \textbf{first of
    the five levels---the Conceptual}: painting the vision through two contrasting images
of the same items. The multi-floor library shows the disentangled form in which
users may walk freely; the multi-galaxy curved universe shows the entangled
native form in which the data arrive. To keep the exposition concrete, from here
on we take the simplest case, in which the only descriptor data given is genre.
We describe the library first, then the universe.

%%% Subsection 3.1 The Multi-Floor Library %%%%%
\subsection{The Multi-Floor Library: The Disentangled Form}\label{subsec:multi_floor_library}

Imagine the user we are designing for. Some of what they want they can
name---a fantasy novel, perhaps, or jazz---a \textit{named}
bearing that points the way in. The rest they cannot: the mood that would
resonate today, the shade of melancholy or wonder they will know only on
contact. Often no precise question arrives at all; ``is there anything
interesting today?'' is enough. Whatever sense of ``their kind of book'' they
carry is felt rather than spoken. A space that serves such a user cannot rely
on what can be named; it must let them move on their own, finding what they
could not say in advance. Three requirements on the space follow.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{(R1) Tangible and locatable:} users can feel the wordless
          texture of the items where they stand, and sense which crossing of
          the multi-dimensional meaning-space they occupy.
    \item \textbf{(R2) Separable and foreseeable:} from that point, users can
          tell what each direction holds---moving that way, they foresee what
          changes and what stays. What varies and what holds are separated.
    \item \textbf{(R3) Steerable and traversable:} with their coordinates and
          the axes as guides, users choose their own direction and roam the
          space at will.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:two_views}a shows a structure that meets all three: imagine a
children's library laid out across many floors. Each \textit{floor} gathers one
genre---fantasy on one, realistic fiction on another, historical narratives on
yet another---and floors of kindred feeling sit near one another, so that the
stack runs without a break. Within each floor, books are
arranged by a closeness one feels but cannot name: on the
fantasy floor, \textit{The Lord of the Rings} and \textit{The Chronicles of
    Narnia} sit together among sweeping mythological landscapes, while
\textit{Kiki's Delivery Service} and \textit{The Worst Witch} lie nearby, both
following young witches on journeys of growth. Standing among them, the user
feels where they are (R1) and can see where a step along the floor would lead
(R2).

What turns these isolated floors into a connected space is the
\textbf{rabbit hole}. At every point on every floor, a rabbit hole
lies underfoot, like an elevator that opens at any spot on the floor.
Step into it, and one emerges at the corresponding position on
another floor---sharing the same atmosphere, however
distant the genre. From \textit{Kiki's Delivery Service} on the
fantasy floor, a rabbit hole leads to \textit{Anne of Green Gables} on
the realistic floor: both quietly luminous coming-of-age stories,
despite their generic distance. The rabbit hole runs vertically,
perpendicular to the within-floor
arrangement.\footnote{For clarity we depict each rabbit hole as
    one-dimensional; in general it may be two- or higher-dimensional.} And
because such
openings lie everywhere, Alice's plunge is available from any point
in the library (R3), realizing the freedom of self-directed
exploration set out at the start.

This is, concretely, the \textbf{disentangled representation} announced
abstractly in Section~\ref{subsec:disentanglement}: not each book having a
coordinate, but a \textit{coordinate system} pervading the whole library, so
that every step carries meaning. The two explorations of
Section~\ref{sec:serendipity} are now walks one can take. A horizontal walk
within a floor is \textit{genre-fixed}: the genre held, the atmosphere varying
book to book. A descent through a rabbit hole is \textit{atmosphere-fixed}: the
atmosphere held, while what varies is something we might call
\textit{genre-ness}.

Each floor gives its own horizontal axis, each rabbit hole its own vertical one.
When floors and rabbit holes are fully consistent, these align into two axes the
whole library shares. We define the shared horizontal axis---where the same
coordinate means the same atmosphere on every floor---as the \textit{atmosphere
    space}, and the shared vertical axis---where the same coordinate means the
same genre-ness through every rabbit hole---as the \textit{genre-ness space}.
Both are unnamed affects read from the body data, the descriptor only orienting
them; the vertical one is genre-ness, a felt quality on which jazz-ness and
blues-ness lie close, not the genre label. \textit{Distant yet close} is no
longer a paradox; it is the local geometry of the library.




%%% Subsection 3.2 The Multi-Galaxy Curved Universe %%%%%
\subsection{The Multi-Galaxy Curved Universe: The Same Items in Their Native Form}\label{subsec:multi_galaxy}

The library is the form we seek; the data themselves do not arrive in
this form. Each item arrives as \textbf{body data}: raw data
preprocessed---typically embedded into a vector space---giving hundreds or
thousands of coordinates per item. The space these vectors inhabit
(Figure~\ref{fig:two_views}b) is the \textbf{multi-galaxy curved
    universe}: the same items in their native, entangled form. Its
dimensionality alone places it beyond what a human can walk; no one
navigates a several-hundred-dimensional space directly.

That, however, is not the heart of the difficulty. Each item has
coordinates here, but they form no coordinate \textit{system} (R2): the
axes of the body data are artifacts of preprocessing, aligned with neither the
atmosphere nor the genre-ness a user feels, and the space itself is
\textbf{nonlinear}---curved, often twisted---so that straight-line paths carry no
meaning.

Within this space, kindred items are distributed within a nonlinear region---often
approximated by a manifold---jazz in one, classical in another, fantasy in yet
another. We call them \textbf{galaxies}. Each galaxy carries its own curved local
frame; no \textit{atmosphere space} or \textit{genre-ness space} is shared
across them.

Rabbit holes inhabit this universe as well, but only as curved
trajectories between galaxies. The one that would lead from Miles
Davis in the jazz galaxy to Debussy in the classical galaxy is such a
curve: in straight-line distance the two pieces remain far apart,
separated by genre though sharing an atmosphere, while the trajectory that
connects them as
\textit{distant yet close} winds through directions the preprocessing
does not single out. These trajectories are precisely what become
vertical elevators in the library once a proper coordinate system is
imposed. For now, \textit{distant yet close} lives in this universe
without being navigable.


%%% Subsection 3.3 Two Views of One Representation %%%%%
\subsection{Two Views of One Representation}\label{subsec:two_views}

The library and the universe (Figure~\ref{fig:two_views}) are two views of one
structure---the same items, threaded by the same rabbit holes. Strictly, the
library is a low-dimensional manifold distilled from the high-dimensional
universe; what separates them as representations is whether a consistent
coordinate system has been laid over the space. To pass from one to the other is
to find that system---one that runs through all galaxies, aligning each galaxy's
internal arrangement within it. Whether it can be made mathematically precise
and learned from data is the question we now turn to: the next section unites the
two under a single object, \textbf{optimal transport on a fiber bundle
    (OT-FB)}.
