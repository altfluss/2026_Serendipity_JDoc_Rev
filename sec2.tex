\section{Serendipity in Information Systems: Prior Work and Research Challenge}\label{sec:serendipity}

\subsection{Serendipity through the Exploration Process}
\label{subsec:serendipity_process}
\paragraph{Origin.}
The word \textit{serendipity} was coined by Horace Walpole after
\textit{The Three Princes of Serendip}, whose heroes made discoveries
``by accidents and sagacity''~\cite{The-Three-Princes-of-Serendip,Walpole1754}.
From the start, the notion held two things together: a chance encounter,
and the prepared mind that recognizes its significance and turns it into
discovery---as in Pasteur's dictum, ``chance favors the prepared
mind''~\cite{Pasteur1854}. Serendipity is thus not luck befalling a passive
observer but something met halfway by a mind that goes looking. What such a
mind gains is more than a new item: a shift in how one sees a domain.

\paragraph{Paradoxes of serendipity.}
Serendipity has long been described in paired terms that pull against each
other: \textit{unexpected yet relevant}, \textit{surprising yet useful},
\textit{novel yet
    familiar}~\cite{Kotkov2016180,Ziarani2021375,Chen2019,Hasan2023933}. Each
pairing names the same tension from a different angle---a discovery must arrive
from somewhere one was not looking, yet land as something that matters. We read
these as facets of a single relation, which we call \textit{distant yet close}.
Before it appears, the user feels only the distance. Once it appears, it seems
close. Yet even then, they cannot say in what. Recall Miles Davis and
Debussy---categorically far apart, yet sharing a contemplative quality. This
paradox takes on a slightly different meaning in each of the two paradigms we
turn to next.

\paragraph{Two paradigms.}
Serendipity research has pursued two complementary paths, differing in where
they place the user and the system (Figure~\ref{fig:serendipity})~\cite{McCayPeet20181}.
\textbf{Recommendation-based} approaches deliver items that are \textit{unexpected
    yet relevant}~\cite{Kotkov2016180,Ziarani2021375,Fu202529}: the system models the
user and selects, computationally, the items worth surprising them
with~\cite{Shrivastava20231,Hasan2023933,Kunaver2017,Chen2019}. Here the
discovered item is itself the prize---a diamond placed before the user
(Figure~\ref{fig:serendipity}a). \textbf{Exploration-based} approaches instead
give the user a structured space to navigate, and agency reverses: the user
moves, and the system provides the terrain~\cite{Marchionini200641,Thudt20121461,Beale2007ambient}.
Here a discovered item is not the prize but a \textit{key}---its value lies in
the door it opens onto a further world to explore (Figure~\ref{fig:serendipity}b).
Both paths are fertile; we take the second.

\paragraph{A serendipitous process, and what it asks of a space.}
This second path stands on a particular view of serendipity---not as a quality
of outcomes but as a process of experience---which has its own rich body of
work. A recurring finding is that serendipity is not a single lucky moment but a
\textit{process}~\cite{Makri2012part1,McCayPeet20151463,Rubin2011,Busch20241110,Corneli2020}:
an unexpected encounter is noticed, judged worth pursuing, and followed until it
reshapes how one sees a domain. A second is that this process is driven by the
user's own \textit{active exploration}~\cite{Dervin1983,Makri20142179}, which
carries them beyond their \textit{comfort zone}---past habitual categories and
settled preferences~\cite{Lee20154851}.

What such a user needs is a space they can explore on their own. Some of what
they seek they can name: they know \textit{what} it is before they reach it, and
it gives their search a first bearing. The rest they cannot name, and learn
\textit{how} it is only on contact---when they play the track, or open the
book. Yet once touched, it points a new way. A single ranking cannot hold this;
what the user needs is a space with directions to turn along, echoing the
affordances Bj\"orneborn finds in a physical
library~\cite{Bjorneborn20171053}: \textit{diversifiability},
\textit{traversability}, and \textit{sensoriability}. The question this paper
pursues is what such a space asks of the \textit{representation} beneath any
interface.

\paragraph{Descriptor data and body data.}
Information retrieval has long recognized a gap between the \textit{descriptive
    relevance} a system can compute and the \textit{affective relevance} a user
seeks, and asked how to ease that tension~\cite{Saracevic1996201,Cosijn2000533}.
The closeness in \textit{distant yet close} is of this affective kind. An
item's \textit{descriptor data}---its named attributes: genre, period,
author---does not carry it, for a descriptor records only what can be named.
Documentation has refined naming into a
discipline---faceted classification~\cite{Broughton200649}---and pressed it
even toward the felt~\cite{Pejtersen1983230,Cho2023567}; yet however
fine the terms, a remainder stays.
Affective relevance lives instead in the \textit{body data}: the work itself,
the track or the text, dense and whole. That relevance resolves into more than
one separable aspect, Xu showed on the side of users'
cognition~\cite{Xu2007179}; the same, on the side of information systems, is the
question we turn to next.

So the task is to resolve the body data, guided by the descriptor, into two
separable directions of exploration. A descriptor names some attributes and
leaves the rest unnamed; this unnamed remainder we call the \textit{atmosphere}.
One direction is \textit{descriptor-fixed}, visiting various atmospheres with a
genre held fixed; the other is \textit{atmosphere-fixed}, visiting items across
various genres with an atmosphere held fixed.
This resolved space is what we set out to design---a \textit{disentangled
    information representation}, a coordinate system drawn from the body alone,
telling the user which way to turn. It comes first, before any interface; how to
learn it is the question we turn to next.


\subsection{Representation Learning and the Disentanglement Challenge}\label{subsec:disentanglement}
\paragraph{Distant yet close as a disentangled representation.}
The structure we seek is, then, a question of \textit{representation
    learning}~\cite{Bengio20131798}: mapping observations into a space whose
distances, neighborhoods, and paths carry meaning~\cite{Bronstein201718}. What
appeared in Section~\ref{subsec:serendipity_process} as a felt relation,
\textit{distant yet close}, the field of representation learning describes as a
\textit{disentangled representation}~\cite{Higgins2017,Higgins2018}: one in which
the underlying factors of variation align with separable directions of the space.
In our setting these factors are the two we have already met---one
\textit{descriptor-fixed}, the other \textit{atmosphere-fixed}. When the two are
separable, an item can be far in one and near in the other at once. When
they are entangled, the two collapse into a single distance, and the
contemplative closeness between Davis and Debussy is absorbed into their
categorical separation.

\paragraph{The challenge: an appropriate inductive bias.}
Constructing such a representation is a recognized open challenge. In the
unsupervised setting it is provably ill-posed: for any disentangled solution,
infinitely many entangled ones generate the same
observations~\cite{Locatello20197247}. Some \textit{inductive bias}---a
structural assumption about the space---is therefore unavoidable. The challenge
is not to add \textit{some} bias, but to define the \textit{appropriate} one for
\textit{distant yet close}. This is where the present work places itself:
Section~\ref{sec:modeling} develops that bias in terms of optimal transport.
