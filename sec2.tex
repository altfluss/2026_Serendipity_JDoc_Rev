\section{Serendipity in Information Systems: Prior Work and Research Challenge}\label{sec:serendipity}

\subsection{Serendipity, Its Paradoxes, and Where They Live}
\label{subsec:serendipity_process}

\paragraph{Origin.}
The word \textit{serendipity} was coined by Horace Walpole after
\textit{The Three Princes of Serendip}, whose heroes made discoveries
``by accidents and sagacity''~\cite{The-Three-Princes-of-Serendip,Walpole1754}.
From the start, the notion held two things together: a chance encounter,
and the prepared mind that recognizes its significance and turns it into
discovery---as in Pasteur's dictum, ``chance favors the prepared
mind''~\cite{Pasteur1854}. Merton, finding the same pairing at work in
science~\cite{MertonBarber2004}, described the experience of an
``unanticipated, anomalous and strategic datum''~\cite[p.~506]{Merton1948505}:
unanticipated and anomalous against the categories the inquirer brought along;
strategic in a value those categories had no name for yet. Serendipity is thus
not luck befalling a passive observer but something met halfway by a mind that
goes looking. What such a mind gains is more than a new item: a shift in how
one sees a domain.

\paragraph{Paradoxes of serendipity.}
Serendipity has long been described in paired terms that pull against each
other: unexpected and relevant, surprising and useful, novel and
familiar~\cite{Kotkov2016180,Ziarani2021375,Chen2019,Hasan2023933}. Each
pairing names the same tension from a different angle---a discovery must arrive
from somewhere one was not looking, yet land as something that matters. Look at
how the pairs are built, and an asymmetry appears. The first term of
each---unexpected, surprising, novel---measures the encounter against what can
be named in advance: categories, classifications, queries, the organized space
of what one knows to look for. The second---relevant, useful,
familiar---reports a closeness felt on contact, before any of those
vocabularies can state it. We read these as facets of a single relation, which
we call \textit{distant yet close}: explicitly distant in features we
\emph{can name}, yet implicitly close in some aspect of features we
\emph{cannot}. Before it appears, the user feels only the distance. Once it
appears, it seems close. Yet even then, they cannot say in what. This
crossing---named distance, unnamed closeness---is not one description of
serendipity among others; it is, we contend, the core of the paradox itself. Remove the named distance, and the encounter is mere
retrieval; remove the unnamed closeness, and it is mere noise. Recall Miles
Davis and Debussy---categorically far apart, yet sharing a contemplative
quality that listeners recognize at once and can name only with difficulty.

\paragraph{Serendipity as a process.}
A recurring finding of this field---by now a premise to build on rather than a
claim to defend---is that serendipity is not a single lucky moment but a
\textit{process}~\cite{Makri2012part1,McCayPeet20151463,Rubin2011,Busch20241110,Corneli2020}:
an unexpected encounter is noticed, judged worth pursuing, and followed until
it reshapes how one sees a domain. McCay-Peet and Toms give the stages
canonical names---a \textit{trigger} is met, a \textit{connection} made,
\textit{followed up}, and matured into a \textit{valuable
    outcome}~\cite{McCayPeet20151463}---and the process is driven by the user's
own active exploration~\cite{Dervin1983,Makri20142179}, which carries them
beyond their comfort zone: past habitual categories and settled
preferences~\cite{Lee20154851}. Where, in this process, does the crossing
live? At its first moment. Makri and Blandford open the process with a new
connection ``involving a mix of unexpected circumstances and
insight''~\cite{Makri2012part1}---and the two ingredients have distinct
addresses. The unexpectedness belongs to the circumstances: the encounter
arrives from outside what could be named in advance. The insight belongs to
the making of the connection: a relevance felt before it can be said. Two
further details of their model deserve emphasis. The need a connection answers
``may be an unconscious need that might only become apparent at the time the
connection is made''~\cite[p.~692]{Makri2012part1}---one can be seeking
without being able to name what would count as finding. And the judgement that
an experience \emph{was} serendipitous arrives only in ``backward-facing
reflections'' on the unexpectedness and insight involved (p.~692); none of
their interviewees named insight in the moment, though the researchers
identified it in nearly every account. The ingredients of serendipity operate
unnamed, and are named in hindsight.

\paragraph{Two completions.}
Not every serendipity, we should say at once, is this paper's concern. The
anomaly on Fleming's plate arrived unbidden, in an inquiry aimed elsewhere,
and was completed by an act of naming: a new proposition, a new term, a
reordered science. That kind cannot be planned or hosted---on this van Andel
is definitive~\cite{vanAndel1994}. Our concern is the explorer's kind. A
person comes looking, knowing the kind of thing they seek but not its
identity; meets, across a categorical distance, something that resonates; and
follows it. Here the discovery need never be put into words to be complete:
what the explorer gains is not a proposition but a direction they can keep.
The two kinds share the crossing and the process skeleton; they part in what
completes them. In Makri and Blandford's classification space---where
experiences vary in their amounts of unexpectedness, insight and
value~\cite{Makri2012part2}---the explorer's kind occupies a region their own
data already visits: moderately unexpected, moderately insightful, and very
valuable. It is this kind, we will argue, that an information system can do
something for.

\subsection{Two Approaches and a Lineage}
\label{subsec:two_approaches}

Research on serendipity spans a spectrum. At one end stands the phenomenon
itself---its history and its anatomy in human
cognition~\cite{MertonBarber2004,vanAndel1994}. Library and information
science holds the middle: serendipity as lived information
experience~\cite{Erdelez1999,Foster2003321,Makri2012part1}, then as something
environments can be shaped to
favor~\cite{Toms2000,Bjorneborn20171053,McCayPeet20181}. At the far end,
recommender systems research made it fully computational: a property of items,
to be scored and optimized~\cite{Kotkov2016180,Ziarani2021375}. Across this
spectrum, serendipity's presumed home shifts---from the person, to the
encounter, to the environment, to the item. This paper stands in the middle
and carries something from each end: the phenomenon as the near end
understands it, kept whole, brought to the definiteness the far end commands.

\paragraph{Two approaches.}
Within information systems, serendipity research has pursued two
complementary paths, differing in where they place the user and the system
(Figure~\ref{fig:serendipity})~\cite{McCayPeet20181}.
\textbf{Recommendation-based} approaches deliver items that are
\textit{unexpected yet relevant}~\cite{Kotkov2016180,Ziarani2021375,Fu2024}:
the system models the user and selects, computationally, the items worth
surprising them with~\cite{Shrivastava20231,Hasan2023933,Kunaver2017,Chen2019}.
Here the discovered item is itself the prize---a diamond placed before the
user (Figure~\ref{fig:serendipity}a). \textbf{Exploration-based} approaches
instead give the user a structured space to navigate, and agency reverses: the
user moves, and the system provides the
terrain~\cite{Marchionini200641,Thudt20121461,Beale2007ambient}. Here a
discovered item is not the prize but a \textit{key}---its value lies in the
door it opens onto a further world to explore
(Figure~\ref{fig:serendipity}b). Read against the process models, the two
images divide serendipity differently. The diamond treats it as an outcome,
complete on delivery. The key treats it as a beginning---the trigger and the
connection---whose meaning lies in the follow-up, through the door. Both paths
are fertile; we take the second.

\paragraph{A lineage, and what it kept meeting.}
The second path has a lineage, though it has seldom been told as one.
Bawden~\cite{Bawden1986203}, asking what information systems could do for
creativity, placed on the agenda ``representation of information to bring out
analogies, patterns, exceptions''---and could specify no mechanism for it.
Toms~\cite{Toms2000} built and tested an early such system; her readers' best
discoveries arrived through a crude textual similarity, and her report is
candid twice over: ``the triggering mechanism is not fully understood,
although most people could explain the connection,'' and, of one route she
named---reasoning by analogy---``implementation is unknown at the moment.''
The Bohemian Bookshelf~\cite{Thudt20121461} multiplied the doors: five
simultaneous visual adjacencies among books, each built on an explicit
attribute---author, keyword, era, page count, cover colour.
Andr\'e~\textit{et al.}~\cite{Andre2009305}, surveying the field, drew the
line that runs through all of it: systems had learned to occasion chance
encounters, but ``the second half of serendipity---the sagacity and wisdom
needed to make the connection between pieces of information---remains
dependent on the human.'' And from the other approach, the recommender surveys
record the same boundary in their own words: serendipity ``includes an
emotional dimension, which is very subjective,'' so that inferring why a user
finds an item serendipitous stays out of
reach~\cite{Kotkov2016180}; the other surveys
concur~\cite{Ziarani2021375,Chen2019}. Four
decades, four vocabularies---analogy, unexplained connection, sagacity,
emotional dimension---and, we read, one referent: a closeness users reliably
feel across categorical distance, which no named attribute carries. Only
recently has this missing layer begun to be named as
such~\cite{Smets202246}.

\paragraph{What a system can hold.}
These testimonies also settle what a system should---and should not---aspire
to. Serendipity itself happens in the person: the noticing, the connection,
the backward-facing judgement are theirs alone
(Section~\ref{subsec:serendipity_process}), and affordances for serendipity
may be intended by designers yet must remain unplanned for
users~\cite{Bjorneborn20171053}. A system that undertakes to manufacture the
experience has traded the key back for the diamond. What a system \emph{can}
hold is the ground: a space in which triggers can be met, in which the felt
closeness has somewhere to be felt, and in which following it is a walk rather
than a leap. Consider what the explorer needs of such a ground. Some of what
they seek they can name: they know \textit{what} it is before they reach it,
and it gives their search a first bearing. The rest they cannot name, and
learn \textit{how} it is only on contact---when they play the track, or open
the book. Yet once touched, it points a new way. A single ranking cannot hold
this; what the user needs is a space with directions to turn along, echoing
the affordances Bj\"orneborn finds in a physical
library~\cite{Bjorneborn20171053}: \textit{diversifiability},
\textit{traversability}, and \textit{sensoriability}. The question this paper
pursues is what such a space asks of the \textit{representation} beneath any
interface---and why every terrain built so far, however accomplished, has
stopped at the same edge. To answer it, we must set the systems aside and look
at the information itself.

\subsection{Naming: Descriptors, Facets, and the Remainder}
\label{subsec:naming}

Information retrieval has long recognized a gap between the
\textit{descriptive relevance} a system can compute and the \textit{affective
    relevance} a user seeks, and asked how to ease that
tension~\cite{Saracevic1996201,Cosijn2000533}. The closeness in
\textit{distant yet close} is of this affective kind. An item's
\textit{descriptor data}---its named attributes: genre, period, author---does
not carry it, for a descriptor records only what can be named. Documentation
has refined naming into a discipline---faceted
classification~\cite{Broughton200649}---and pressed it even toward the felt:
Pejtersen and Austin, in this journal, gave readers' sought experiences a
controlled vocabulary of their own~\cite{Pejtersen1983230}; Cho
\textit{et al.}, in this journal forty years on, distilled from readers' words
a vocabulary of mood~\cite{Cho2023567}. Yet however fine the terms, a
remainder stays---and it is at the refined edge that the remainder shows most
clearly, reported by the tradition's own hands. Cosijn and Ingwersen, revising
the typology of relevance, found that its affective member would not sit as
one more category, and redrew it as a dimension running through the
others~\cite{Cosijn2000533}. Cho \textit{et al.} record which of their
readers' words had to be set aside: the most personal---\textit{interesting},
\textit{absorbing}---and the most equivocal---\textit{emotional},
\textit{intense}---for want of connection to any one nameable
mood~\cite{Cho2023567}. The remainder, then, is not the defect of a
vocabulary, to be repaired by the next; it is what naming, as such, leaves.
The named side of information is sparse and explicit; the unnamed side is
dense and tacit---we can know more than we can tell~\cite{Polanyi1966}.
Affective relevance lives instead in the \textit{body data}: the work itself,
the track or the text, dense and whole. That relevance resolves into more than
one separable aspect, Xu showed on the side of users'
cognition~\cite{Xu2007179}; the same, on the side of information
representation, is the question we now make precise.

And here is why the lineage of Section~\ref{subsec:two_approaches} kept
meeting the same edge. A space organized by descriptors alone---however many
facets it multiplies---renders the named distance of the crossing, but gives
the felt closeness no axis to live on: the resonance is in the works, not in
the layout, and the pursuit has no road. A space that dissolves everything
into a single similarity---as the embedding lineage of recommenders
does---contains the unnamed but entangles it with the named: an item distant
in genre and close in feeling is simply, on balance, distant, and the
direction that holds the feeling while crossing the category cannot even be
stated. One kind of space lacks the unnamed; the other lacks the separation.
So the task is to resolve the body data, guided by the descriptor, into two
separable directions of exploration. A descriptor names some attributes and
leaves the rest unnamed; this unnamed remainder we call the
\textit{atmosphere}. One direction is \textit{descriptor-fixed}, visiting
various atmospheres with a genre held fixed; the other is
\textit{atmosphere-fixed}, visiting items across various genres with an
atmosphere held fixed. For the distant-yet-close kind of serendipity, a
representation that holds these two apart---readable at every point, at
once---is not one useful feature among others: it is the precondition for the
process to have anywhere to run. This resolved space is what we set out to
design---a \textit{disentangled information representation}, a coordinate
system drawn from the body alone, telling the user which way to turn. It comes
first, before any interface; how to learn it is the question we turn to next.

\subsection{Representation Learning and the Disentanglement Challenge}
\label{subsec:disentanglement}

The structure we seek is, then, a question of \textit{representation
    learning}~\cite{Bengio20131798}: mapping observations into a space whose
distances, neighborhoods, and paths carry meaning~\cite{Bronstein201718}. What
appeared above as a felt relation, \textit{distant yet close}, the field of
representation learning describes as a \textit{disentangled
    representation}~\cite{Higgins2017,Higgins2018}: one in which the underlying
factors of variation align with separable directions of the space. In our
setting these factors are the two we have already met---one
\textit{descriptor-fixed}, the other \textit{atmosphere-fixed}. When the two
are separable, an item can be far in one and near in the other at once. When
they are entangled, the two collapse into a single distance, and the
contemplative closeness between Davis and Debussy is absorbed into their
categorical separation.

Constructing such a representation is a recognized open challenge. In the
unsupervised setting it is provably ill-posed: for any disentangled solution,
infinitely many entangled ones generate the same
observations~\cite{Locatello20197247}. Some \textit{inductive bias}---a
structural assumption about the space---is therefore unavoidable. The
challenge is not to add \textit{some} bias, but to define the
\textit{appropriate} one for \textit{distant yet close}. And one requirement
on that bias follows from everything above: it cannot itself be a naming.
Specify the atmosphere in named terms, and we have merely extended the
descriptor---and recreated the remainder one step further out. What is needed
is a structural assumption that determines the unnamed axis without naming
it---and that, for the same reason, decides nothing about what any user will
find there, or what will resonate. The ground does not anticipate the
discovery. This is where the present work places itself:
Section~\ref{sec:modeling} develops that bias in terms of optimal transport.

The \textit{atmosphere-fixed} direction the user follows---crossing
descriptors while holding an atmosphere---is what we call a \textit{rabbit
    hole}: among the infinitely many such directions, the one that carries
meaning. Finding it is the work we take up---its vision in
Section~\ref{sec:library_universe_model}, its mathematics in
Section~\ref{sec:modeling}, its learning from data in
Section~\ref{sec:computational}.
