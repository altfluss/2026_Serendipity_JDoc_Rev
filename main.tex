\begin{abstract}\noindent
    Serendipity is often described as finding something ``unexpected yet relevant.''
    We read this as the \textit{distant-yet-close paradox}: items explicitly distant
    in named features yet implicitly close in unnamed ones. This is a problem of
    \textit{representation}, not of interface design: the paradox can be addressed
    only in a space that disentangles the two senses of closeness. Disentangled
    representation, however, is ill-posed; the right one is singled out by an
    \textit{inductive bias} suited to what one wants. We propose \textit{optimal
        transport} as that bias, and the representation it yields is the
    \textbf{Optimal Transport Fiber Bundle (OT-FB)}: genre-ness and atmosphere
    coordinates form separable axes of one geometry, connected by \textit{rabbit
        holes} between genre-distant yet atmosphere-close regions. Two
    views render this one structure: the \textbf{multi-galaxy curved universe}, the
    entangled form in which data arrive, and the \textbf{multi-floor library}, the
    disentangled form in which users may walk. We pursue this representation through
    five inseparable levels---conceptual, mathematical, computational,
    implementational, and empirical. Participants explored across genres while
    staying along an atmosphere axis, and described discovering not only items
    but also their own preferences. The contribution is the disentangled
    representation itself, given well-defined form: distant yet close ceases to be
    a paradox and becomes the local geometry of the bundle.
\end{abstract}

\begin{quote}\parindent=0pt\parskip=.5em\noindent
    \begin{center}
        \textbf{Structured Abstract}
    \end{center}
    \textbf{Purpose.}
    We address the \textit{distant-yet-close paradox} of serendipity---items
    explicitly distant in named features yet implicitly close in unnamed
    ones---as a problem of \textit{information representation} rather than
    interface design. Our aim is to construct, from data, a \textit{disentangled
        representation space} in which this paradox becomes navigable.

    \textbf{Design/methodology/approach.}
    Disentangled representation is ill-posed; the right one is singled out by an
    \textit{inductive bias} suited to what one wants. We propose the
    \textit{optimal transport (OT) hypothesis} as that bias, aligning the
    atmosphere coordinate across genres. Under this hypothesis the
    representation takes the form of an \textbf{Optimal Transport Fiber Bundle
        (OT-FB)}, in which genre-ness and atmosphere coordinates form separable
    axes of one geometry, connected by \textit{rabbit holes}. We pursue this
    representation through five inseparable levels---\textbf{conceptual}
    (multi-galaxy curved universe, multi-floor library), \textbf{mathematical}
    (OT-FB), \textbf{computational} (learning the bundle from data),
    \textbf{implementational} (rendering the library as interface), and
    \textbf{empirical} (a user study with 135 participants exploring music).

    \textbf{Findings.}
    The OT-FB learned from the Million Song Dataset arranged ten genres along a
    common atmosphere coordinate independent of any genre. In the user study, participants
    explored more broadly across genres while staying along an atmosphere axis,
    and described discovering not only items but also that their preferences
    lived along that axis.

    \textbf{Originality/value.}
    The contribution is the disentangled representation itself, given well-defined
    mathematical form through the OT hypothesis and realized coherently from
    concept to user experience. With this representation in place, distant yet
    close ceases to be a paradox and becomes the local geometry of the bundle.
\end{quote}
